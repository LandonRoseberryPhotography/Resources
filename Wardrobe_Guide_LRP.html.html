Actor Headshot Prep

Everything you wear
is a reference.

There is nothing new under the sun. A suit references businessmen. A flannel references blue-collar workers. A leather jacket references rebels. You cannot put on a piece of clothing without pointing toward an archetype — toward a type of person, a world, a story.

We're not inventing anything. We're being intentional about which reference you're already making — and making sure every single thing you wear is pointing in the same direction. The goal: anyone who looks at your headshot knows exactly who you are with zero conflicting information.

"

My acting teacher told me: an audition is a crime scene. The casting director is a detective trying to find who did it. Everyone who walks in has a little blood on their shoes, a little on their knees — but you need to walk in head to toe dripping blood saying, "I killed them."

— Jacob Elordi

02

Intentional Inspiration

On stage and on film, there is always a costume designer. Their entire job is to make sure the character's visual personality is readable to the audience before a single word is spoken. That's exactly what we're doing here — without the budget.

Go to Google Images or Pinterest and search for characters, actors, or editorials that match the type you're going for. Study the wardrobe. Take Tom Holland — fun, upbeat, boy next door energy. Pull screencaps from his films and look at how every piece of clothing is an extension of that character's personality. Nothing is accidental. Nothing is neutral.

04

Your coloring

Answer both questions below — your undertone and your contrast level together determine which colors will work hardest for you. The recommendations won't appear until both are selected.

Step 1 — What are your undertones?

Look at the inside of your wrist in natural light. If your veins appear greenish, you lean warm. If they appear blue or purple, you lean cool. If you honestly can't tell, you're likely neutral. Warm skin has golden or peachy hues; cool skin has pink or rosy hues.

Warm
Golden, peachy, or yellow undertones. Skin glows in sunlight. Veins appear greenish. Tans easily.
Cool
Pink, rosy, or red undertones. Skin flushes easily. Veins appear blue or purple. May burn before tanning.
Neutral
A blend of warm and cool. Veins appear blue-green. Both gold and silver jewelry look good on you.
Step 2 — What is your contrast level?

Look at the difference between your skin, hair, and eyes. Light blonde hair with fair skin = low contrast. Dark hair against fair skin, or rich deep complexion with striking eyes = high contrast.

Low Contrast
Skin, hair, and eyes are close in value. Features blend gently together. Think: golden blonde with fair skin, or soft gray hair with light complexion.
Medium Contrast
Moderate difference between your features. Medium tones throughout — not dramatically light or dark. Most people fall here.
High Contrast
Strong difference between skin and hair — dark hair against fair skin, or a deep complexion with striking light eyes. Your coloring makes a statement on its own.

✓

✓
